<div className="radius-header">
  <h1 className="radius-title">Shape, With Intent</h1>
  <p className="radius-description">
    A corner is a decision about personality. Sharp reads precise and technical, fully round reads soft and playful, and the space in between reads considered. Premium products almost never live at the extremes. They pick one moderate signature radius, apply it with discipline, and let a small scale handle everything else. This system does the same: a <code>6px</code> signature on every control, a short ladder up to hero surfaces, and the fully round pill kept back as an accent, never a default.
  </p>
</div>

<div className="radius-section">
  <h2 className="radius-section-title">Why 6px Is the Signature</h2>
  <p className="radius-lede">
    Rounder is not more premium. What reads as expensive is consistency and restraint: a single control radius repeated everywhere, and shape that scales with the size of the surface. So <code>6px</code> is the signature control radius on every button, input, and select, the same value the Vercel Geist system uses on its controls, and the fully round pill stays an accent rather than a default. When shape tracks the role and size of a surface, the whole interface reads as one intentional system.
  </p>
</div>

<div className="radius-section">
  <h2 className="radius-section-title">The Golden Rule</h2>

  <div className="golden">
    <div className="golden-kicker">Read this before you round anything</div>
    <div className="golden-headline">
      The pill is an accent, not a default. Buttons are not pills. Scale the radius to the size and role of the element.
    </div>
    <p className="golden-body">
      A fully rounded corner is a strong statement, and a statement repeated everywhere stops meaning anything. Reserve <code>--radius-pill</code> for small, single-line elements where the round shape is the point: chips, tags, avatars, toggles, icon buttons, one-line status badges. A standard button gets the <code>6px</code> signature, not the pill. Beyond that, bigger surfaces earn bigger radii: a card is not shaped like a button, and a modal is not shaped like a card. When shape tracks size, the whole interface reads as one intentional system.
    </p>

<div className="radius-section">
  <h2 className="radius-section-title">The Law of Concentric Corners</h2>
  <p className="radius-lede">
    When one rounded element sits inside another, the corners should be concentric: parallel curves that share a center, like the gap between a phone screen and its housing. The rule is simple arithmetic. The inner radius equals the outer radius minus the padding between them.
  </p>

  <div className="formula">inner radius = outer radius &minus; padding</div>

  <p className="radius-lede">
    This is not folklore. Apple formalized it in SwiftUI with <code>ConcentricRectangle</code> and the <code>containerShape</code> modifier, where a nested shape derives its radius from the container and the padding automatically. Skip the math and the inner corner either bulges past the outer curve or leaves an awkward pinch where the two arcs fight. Below, both cards use <code>--radius-xl</code> (16px) on the outside with 8px of padding. On the left the inner surface is set to <code>--radius-md</code> (16 minus 8), so the corners run parallel. On the right the inner surface keeps the full 16px and the curves collide.
  </p>

  <div className="concentric-stage">
    <div className="concentric-outer right">
      <div className="concentric-inner">inner 8px = 16px &minus; 8px padding</div>
    </div>
    <div className="concentric-label right">
      <strong>Concentric</strong>
      Inner radius reduced by the padding. Curves stay parallel, corners feel machined.
    </div>
    <div className="concentric-outer wrong">
      <div className="concentric-inner">inner 16px, ignores the padding</div>
    </div>
    <div className="concentric-label wrong">
      <strong>Fighting</strong>
      Inner radius left at the outer value. The two arcs pinch and the nesting looks loose.
    </div>
  </div>
</div>

<div className="radius-section">
  <h2 className="radius-section-title">Continuous Corners, When the Browser Allows</h2>
  <p className="radius-lede">
    A standard CSS rounded corner is a quarter of a circle: the straight edge meets the arc abruptly, and the curvature jumps from zero to its maximum at a single point. Apple's interfaces round corners a different way. A <code>RoundedRectangle(cornerRadius:style:.continuous)</code> in SwiftUI draws a squircle, where the curvature eases in and out so the transition from edge to corner is smooth. The eye reads that gradual onset as softer and more refined, even when the nominal radius is identical. It is a large part of why native Apple surfaces feel expensive.
  </p>
  <p className="radius-lede">
    The web is catching up. The CSS <code>corner-shape</code> property lets a corner follow a superellipse instead of a circular arc, and <code>corner-shape: squircle</code> is defined as equivalent to <code>superellipse(2)</code>. We adopt it as progressive enhancement: every element ships a normal <code>border-radius</code> that works everywhere, and where the browser supports it we upgrade the corner to a squircle inside an <code>@supports</code> query. No fallback is ever broken, and supporting browsers get the smoother curve for free.
  </p>

  <div className="squircle-stage">
    <div className="squircle-card">
      <div className="squircle-shape arc"></div>
      <div className="squircle-name">Circular arc</div>
      <p className="squircle-text">The universal fallback. Plain <code>border-radius</code>, a quarter-circle corner. Renders identically in every browser.</p>
    </div>
    <div className="squircle-card">
      <div className="squircle-shape continuous"></div>
      <div className="squircle-name">Continuous (squircle)</div>
      <p className="squircle-text">Same nominal radius, upgraded with <code>corner-shape: squircle</code> where supported. If your browser lacks it, this shape falls back to the arc on the left, which is the point.</p>
    </div>
  </div>

  <div className="radius-note">
    <div className="radius-note-title">Honest about support</div>
    <div className="radius-note-body">
      <code>corner-shape</code> is new and support is limited today, landing first in recent Chromium. That is exactly why it is an enhancement and not a requirement. Never gate layout, hit targets, or legibility on it. The base <code>border-radius</code> carries the design, and the squircle is a quiet reward for browsers that can render it.
    </div>
  </div>
</div>

<div className="radius-section">
  <h2 className="radius-section-title">Rules of Implementation</h2>

  <div className="rules-grid">
    <div className="rules-col do">
      <div className="rules-col-title"><span className="rules-badge">+</span> Do</div>
      <ul className="rules-list">
        <li>Give every button, input, and select the <code>sm</code> signature, 6px.</li>
        <li>Use <code>lg</code> (12px) for cards, menus, and popovers.</li>
        <li>Use <code>xl</code> (16px) for modals and sheets.</li>
        <li>Reserve <code>pill</code> for small one-line accents: chips, tags, avatars, toggles.</li>
        <li>Scale radius up with surface size, so shape tracks role.</li>
        <li>Compute nested corners as <code>inner = outer - padding</code>.</li>
        <li>Reference the tokens by name; never hardcode a pixel radius.</li>
      </ul>
    </div>
    <div className="rules-col dont">
      <div className="rules-col-title"><span className="rules-badge">x</span> Don't</div>
      <ul className="rules-list">
        <li>Shape a wide button or a card as a pill.</li>
        <li>Use a radius that is not on the scale.</li>
        <li>Reuse one radius on every surface regardless of size.</li>
        <li>Leave a nested inner corner at the outer radius.</li>
        <li>Treat rounder as automatically more premium.</li>
        <li>Gate layout or hit targets on <code>corner-shape</code> support.</li>
      </ul>
    </div>
  </div>
</div>

    <div className="decision-step">

    </div>
