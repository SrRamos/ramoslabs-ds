    <h1 className="mo-title">Motion With a Job to Do</h1>
    <p className="mo-description">
      Motion in this system is a tool, not a garnish. Every animation exists to answer one of three questions for the person using the product: where did that come from, how are these two states related, and did my action register. If a movement cannot claim one of those jobs, it does not ship. The whole system is small on purpose: three durations and five easings, each with a defined role, so that motion stays consistent, fast, and quiet.
    </p>
  </div>

<div className="mo-section">
  <h2 className="mo-section-title">Purposeful, Not Decorative</h2>
  <p className="mo-lede">
    Good motion orients the user. It carries the eye from a tapped button to the panel that opened, it shows that a list item slid rather than teleported, and it confirms that a request was received. That is motion doing informational work: orientation, continuity, and feedback. Decorative motion does none of this. It draws attention to itself, delays the interface, and costs the same battery and main-thread time as motion that means something. The rule is simple: motion earns its place by clarifying a change, never by dressing one up.
  </p>

  <div className="mo-purpose">
    <div className="mo-purpose-card keep">
      <div className="mo-purpose-tag"><span className="mo-symbol">check_circle</span> Motion that has a job</div>
      <ul className="mo-purpose-list">
        <li><strong>Orientation.</strong> A menu grows from the button that opened it, so the eye knows where it came from.</li>
        <li><strong>Continuity.</strong> A row animates to its new position instead of jumping, so the change reads as one object moving.</li>
        <li><strong>Feedback.</strong> A control settles after a tap, confirming the press was received.</li>
      </ul>
    </div>
    <div className="mo-purpose-card">
      <div className="mo-purpose-tag"><span className="mo-symbol">cancel</span> Motion to cut</div>
      <ul className="mo-purpose-list">
        <li>Entrances that animate for their own sake with nothing to orient.</li>
        <li>Looping, attention-grabbing movement with no status behind it.</li>
        <li>Long, cinematic reveals that make the interface wait on the animation.</li>
      </ul>
    </div>
  </div>
</div>

<div className="mo-section">
  <h2 className="mo-section-title">The Five Easings, and When Each One Fits</h2>
  <p className="mo-lede">
    Easing is the shape of the speed over time, and it is where motion communicates. A curve that starts fast and settles reads as something arriving. A curve that starts slow and accelerates reads as something leaving. Choosing the wrong one is not a matter of polish, it makes the interface feel laggy or mechanical. Each easing below has one job.
  </p>

  <div className="mo-easings">

<div className="mo-section">
  <h2 className="mo-section-title">Performance Is Part of the Contract</h2>
  <p className="mo-lede">
    A frame budget at 60fps is about 16ms. Miss it and motion visibly stutters. The browser can animate two properties, <span className="mo-inline-code">transform</span> and <span className="mo-inline-code">opacity</span>, on the compositor thread, off the main thread, without recalculating layout or repainting. Animating geometry instead, <span className="mo-inline-code">width</span>, <span className="mo-inline-code">height</span>, <span className="mo-inline-code">top</span>, <span className="mo-inline-code">left</span>, or <span className="mo-inline-code">margin</span>, forces the browser to recompute layout on every frame. That is layout thrash, and it is the usual cause of janky animation. This is not a preference, it is how the rendering pipeline works, so the system animates transform and opacity by default and treats anything else as a decision that needs a reason.
  </p>

  <div className="mo-perf">
    <div className="mo-perf-card cheap">
      <div className="mo-perf-head"><span className="mo-symbol">bolt</span> Compositor cheap</div>
      <div className="mo-perf-props">
        <span className="mo-chip">transform</span>
        <span className="mo-chip">opacity</span>
      </div>
      <p className="mo-perf-text">Handled on the compositor, off the main thread. No layout, no paint. To move, scale, or fade, reach for these. A slide is <span style={{ fontFamily: "'SF Mono', monospace" }}>translateY</span>, not a change to <span style={{ fontFamily: "'SF Mono', monospace" }}>top</span>.</p>
    </div>
    <div className="mo-perf-card">
      <div className="mo-perf-head"><span className="mo-symbol">warning</span> Forces layout</div>
      <div className="mo-perf-props">
        <span className="mo-chip">width</span>
        <span className="mo-chip">height</span>
        <span className="mo-chip">top</span>
        <span className="mo-chip">left</span>
        <span className="mo-chip">margin</span>
      </div>
      <p className="mo-perf-text">Each animated frame recomputes layout for the page, then repaints. On a long list or a weak device this drops frames. Avoid animating these, express the same motion with transform.</p>
    </div>
  </div>

  <div className="mo-note">
    <span className="mo-symbol" aria-hidden="true">tips_and_updates</span>
    <div>
      <div className="mo-note-title">Use will-change sparingly</div>
      <div className="mo-note-body">
        <code>will-change: transform</code> can hint the browser to promote an element to its own layer before it animates, but a layer costs memory. Apply it just before the animation and remove it after, and never blanket it across many elements. If you are already animating transform and opacity, you rarely need it at all.
      </div>
    </div>
  </div>
</div>

<div className="mo-section">
  <h2 className="mo-section-title">Reduced Motion Is the Floor, Not a Feature</h2>
  <p className="mo-lede">
    Some people feel physically ill from movement on a screen. Their operating system carries a setting for this, and the browser exposes it as <span className="mo-inline-code">prefers-reduced-motion: reduce</span>. A value of <span className="mo-inline-code">reduce</span> is a firm request to hold still, and honoring it is a baseline every animation clears, never an enhancement bolted on later. This aligns with WCAG 2.1: <strong>2.3.3 Animation from Interactions</strong> asks that motion triggered by interaction can be disabled, and <strong>2.2.2 Pause, Stop, Hide</strong> covers looping motion.
  </p>
  <p className="mo-lede">
    Honoring it does not mean deleting meaning. Because information rides on color, opacity, and the final state rather than on the movement itself, the interface stays fully legible without motion. Collapse the animated part, keep the destination. And keep a spinner turning slowly rather than freezing it, a stopped spinner reads as a hung app. Reduce the motion, do not remove the signal.
  </p>

  <div className="mo-code">
    <div className="mo-code-header">
      <span className="mo-code-dot red" aria-hidden="true"></span>
      <span className="mo-code-dot yellow" aria-hidden="true"></span>

      <span className="mo-code-lang">CSS</span>
    </div>

<div className="mo-section">
  <h2 className="mo-section-title">Rules of Motion</h2>

  <div className="mo-rules-grid">
    <div className="mo-rules-col do">
      <div className="mo-rules-col-title"><span className="mo-rules-badge">+</span> Do</div>
      <ul className="mo-rules-list">
        <li>Give every animation a job: orientation, continuity, or feedback.</li>
        <li>Animate <code>transform</code> and <code>opacity</code> so the compositor carries the work.</li>
        <li>Name the property, a duration token, and an easing token explicitly.</li>
        <li>Match duration to the change: <code>fast</code> local, <code>normal</code> to enter or leave, <code>slow</code> for a takeover.</li>
        <li>Ease <code>out</code> to enter, <code>in</code> to leave, <code>in-out</code> to move, <code>linear</code> to loop.</li>
        <li>Reserve <code>spring</code> for a small, discrete success, and gate it behind reduced motion.</li>
        <li>Honor <code>prefers-reduced-motion</code> as a floor: collapse the motion, keep the meaning.</li>
      </ul>
    </div>
    <div className="mo-rules-col dont">
      <div className="mo-rules-col-title"><span className="mo-rules-badge">x</span> Don't</div>
      <ul className="mo-rules-list">
        <li>Add motion with no job. Decoration is the thing to cut.</li>
        <li>Animate <code>width</code>, <code>height</code>, <code>top</code>, <code>left</code>, or <code>margin</code>. That is layout thrash.</li>
        <li>Reach for <code>transition: all</code>. Name the property.</li>
        <li>Run anything longer than <code>300ms</code>. Longer reads as sluggish.</li>
        <li>Use <code>linear</code> on anything with a start and an end.</li>
        <li>Spring a large surface. An overshooting modal looks broken.</li>
        <li>Let motion alone carry meaning. It must survive reduced motion.</li>
      </ul>
    </div>
  </div>
</div>
