        <div className="pers-step done"></div>

        <div className="pers-step"></div>

  <div className="pers-line">

  </div>

{/* ============================================ */}
{/* 3. RECIPROCITY                               */}
{/* ============================================ */}

<div className="pers-principle">
  <div className="pers-principle-head">
    <span className="pers-num">3</span>
    <h2 className="pers-principle-title">Reciprocity</h2>
  </div>

  <p className="pers-lede">
    People return a favor. Deliver something genuinely useful first, then make a fair ask, and it lands on willing ground rather than a cold wall. <strong>The value stays whether or not they say yes.</strong>
  </p>

  <div className="pers-rule">
    <p className="pers-rule-line"><span className="pers-rule-key">Rule</span> Deliver a genuinely useful result first, then make a fair ask the user is free to decline.</p>
    <p className="pers-rule-line"><span className="pers-rule-key">Why</span> Value given up front lands the request on willing ground and keeps its worth either way.</p>
  </div>

  <div className="pers-evidence">
    <span className="pers-material" aria-hidden="true">science</span>
    <div className="pers-evidence-body">
      <strong>An unsolicited favor creates a felt obligation to return it.</strong> In Regan's classic experiment, people given a small unrequested gift later agreed to a larger request far more often than a control group.
      <span className="pers-cite">Regan (1971); Cialdini (1984). Notes below.</span>
    </div>
  </div>

  <div className="pers-apply">
    <div className="pers-apply-title"><span className="pers-material" aria-hidden="true">redeem</span> In a RamosLabs product</div>
    <p className="pers-apply-text">
      Let the product prove itself before it asks. Return a real result, then invite the user to save it. The request comes after the gift, never as a toll gate in front of it.
    </p>
    <div className="pers-demo">
      <div className="pers-price-offer">
        <div className="pers-price-cap">Your result, ready</div>
        <div className="pers-price-value">Report generated</div>
        <p className="pers-price-note">Yours to read now. Create a free account only if you want to save it.</p>
      </div>
    </div>
  </div>

  <div className="pers-line">
    <div className="pers-line-col ok">
      <div className="pers-line-tag"><span className="pers-material" aria-hidden="true">check_circle</span> Recommended</div>
      <p className="pers-line-text">Give a genuinely useful result up front, then ask to save or continue. <strong>The gift keeps its value even if the user declines.</strong></p>
    </div>
    <div className="pers-line-col avoid">
      <div className="pers-line-tag"><span className="pers-material" aria-hidden="true">cancel</span> Avoid</div>
      <p className="pers-line-text">A hollow "free" gift that is really a hook, or a preview that vanishes unless you pay. <strong>If the value evaporates the moment the user hesitates, it was bait.</strong></p>
    </div>
  </div>
</div>

{/* ============================================ */}
{/* 4. IKEA / ENDOWMENT EFFECT                   */}
{/* ============================================ */}

<div className="pers-principle">
  <div className="pers-principle-head">
    <span className="pers-num">4</span>
    <h2 className="pers-principle-title">The IKEA and Endowment Effect</h2>
  </div>

  <p className="pers-lede">
    We value what we build and own more than the identical thing untouched. Let a person name a project or arrange a layout and it becomes theirs in their mind before it is theirs in fact, so signing up feels like <strong>keeping something they already have</strong>.
  </p>

  <div className="pers-rule">
    <p className="pers-rule-line"><span className="pers-rule-key">Rule</span> Let the user personalize real work before any gate, then carry that work intact through sign-up.</p>
    <p className="pers-rule-line"><span className="pers-rule-key">Why</span> People value what they build, so keeping their own work feels like keeping something they own.</p>
  </div>

  <div className="pers-evidence">
    <span className="pers-material" aria-hidden="true">science</span>
    <div className="pers-evidence-body">
      <strong>Labor and ownership both inflate perceived value.</strong> People price self-assembled goods above identical ready-made ones (the IKEA effect), and owners demand more to give up an object than others will pay for it (the endowment effect).
      <span className="pers-cite">Norton, Mochon &amp; Ariely (2012); Kahneman, Knetsch &amp; Thaler (1990). Notes below.</span>
    </div>
  </div>

  <div className="pers-apply">
    <div className="pers-apply-title"><span className="pers-material" aria-hidden="true">build</span> In a RamosLabs product</div>
    <p className="pers-apply-text">
      Let the user personalize before the gate, then carry that work intact through sign-up so nothing they made is lost. Investment they chose to make is legitimate; investment tricked out of them is not.
    </p>
    <div className="pers-demo">
      <label className="pers-field-label" htmlFor="pers-project">Name your workspace</label>
      <input id="pers-project" className="pers-field filled" defaultValue="Q3 launch plan" />
      <label className="pers-field-label" htmlFor="pers-layout">Starting layout</label>
      <div className="pers-opts" id="pers-layout">
        <span className="pers-opt sel">Board</span>
        <span className="pers-opt">List</span>
        <span className="pers-opt">Timeline</span>
      </div>
    </div>
  </div>

  <div className="pers-line">
    <div className="pers-line-col ok">
      <div className="pers-line-tag"><span className="pers-material" aria-hidden="true">check_circle</span> Recommended</div>
      <p className="pers-line-text">Offer real customization the user wants, and carry their work across the sign-up boundary intact. <strong>They keep exactly what they built.</strong></p>
    </div>
    <div className="pers-line-col avoid">
      <div className="pers-line-tag"><span className="pers-material" aria-hidden="true">cancel</span> Avoid</div>
      <p className="pers-line-text">Forcing busywork to manufacture sunk cost, then holding the user's own work hostage behind a paywall. <strong>Effort must never become a hostage.</strong></p>
    </div>
  </div>
</div>

{/* ============================================ */}
{/* 5. LOSS AVERSION (CONTESTED)                 */}
{/* ============================================ */}

<div className="pers-principle">
  <div className="pers-principle-head">
    <span className="pers-num">5</span>
    <h2 className="pers-principle-title">Loss Aversion <span className="pers-contested">Contested</span></h2>
  </div>

  <p className="pers-lede">
    A loss tends to weigh heavier than an equivalent gain, so framing a choice around what a person stands to lose can sharpen attention. Use it only where a <strong>real loss exists</strong>: an unsaved draft, an earned credit about to expire. Never invent one.
  </p>

  <div className="pers-rule">
    <p className="pers-rule-line"><span className="pers-rule-key">Rule</span> Frame a choice around loss only where a real, preventable loss exists, and pair the warning with the fix.</p>
    <p className="pers-rule-line"><span className="pers-rule-key">Why</span> A true loss sharpens attention honestly, while an invented one is manufactured fear.</p>
  </div>

  <div className="pers-evidence">
    <span className="pers-material" aria-hidden="true">science</span>
    <div className="pers-evidence-body">
      <strong>Losses loom larger than gains.</strong> Prospect theory formalized it: the value function is steeper for losses than for equal-sized gains. The size of that asymmetry, though, is actively debated.
      <span className="pers-cite">Kahneman &amp; Tversky (1979); Gal &amp; Rucker (2018). Notes below.</span>
    </div>
  </div>

  <div className="pers-apply">
    <div className="pers-apply-title"><span className="pers-material" aria-hidden="true">savings</span> In a RamosLabs product</div>
    <p className="pers-apply-text">
      Name the real thing at risk, plainly, and pair the warning with an easy way to prevent the loss. No invented deadlines, no fake stock counters.
    </p>
    <div className="pers-demo">
      <div className="pers-price-cap" style={{ color: '#4f46e5' }}>Honest loss-framed reminder</div>
      <p className="pers-apply-text" style={{ marginTop: '0.35rem' }}>
        You have <strong>3 unsaved sections</strong>. Create a free account to keep them, or they clear when this tab closes.
      </p>
    </div>
  </div>

  <div className="pers-line">
    <div className="pers-line-col ok">
      <div className="pers-line-tag"><span className="pers-material" aria-hidden="true">check_circle</span> Recommended</div>
      <p className="pers-line-text">Remind the user of value that truly exists and truly could be lost, with a clear way to keep it. <strong>The loss is real and preventable.</strong></p>
    </div>
    <div className="pers-line-col avoid">
      <div className="pers-line-tag"><span className="pers-material" aria-hidden="true">cancel</span> Avoid</div>
      <p className="pers-line-text">Fake "only 2 left" counters, invented countdowns, or confirmshaming ("No thanks, I like overpaying"). <strong>Manufactured fear is the sharpest dark pattern of all, and it is banned.</strong></p>
    </div>
  </div>
</div>

{/* ============================================ */}
{/* 6. CONTRAST EFFECT                           */}
{/* ============================================ */}

<div className="pers-principle">
  <div className="pers-principle-head">
    <span className="pers-num">6</span>
    <h2 className="pers-principle-title">Contrast Effect</h2>
  </div>

  <p className="pers-lede">
    A number means little in isolation. Beside a true reference, a real list price, a genuine alternative, or the value it returns, the same cost reads as proportionate. Anchor only on a <strong>reference the user could verify</strong>.
  </p>

  <div className="pers-rule">
    <p className="pers-rule-line"><span className="pers-rule-key">Rule</span> Show every price beside a reference the user could independently verify.</p>
    <p className="pers-rule-line"><span className="pers-rule-key">Why</span> A number reads as proportionate next to a true comparison, and every figure on screen stays checkable.</p>
  </div>

  <div className="pers-evidence">
    <span className="pers-material" aria-hidden="true">science</span>
    <div className="pers-evidence-body">
      <strong>Judgments latch onto whatever reference is offered.</strong> An anchor pulls numeric estimates toward it even when people know it is arbitrary, and it shifts what they will actually pay for a real product.
      <span className="pers-cite">Tversky &amp; Kahneman (1974); Ariely, Loewenstein &amp; Prelec (2003). Notes below.</span>
    </div>
  </div>

  <div className="pers-apply">
    <div className="pers-apply-title"><span className="pers-material" aria-hidden="true">payments</span> In a RamosLabs product</div>
    <p className="pers-apply-text">
      Never show a price bare. Set it beside a genuine reference on a <code>--color-surface-secondary</code> card, with the offer on a <code>--color-primary</code> bordered card. Let the comparison inform, not corner.
    </p>
    <div className="pers-demo">
      <div className="pers-price-row">
        <div className="pers-price-anchor">
          <div className="pers-price-cap">What it replaces</div>
          <div className="pers-price-value">$180 / mo</div>
          <p className="pers-price-note">Three separate tools you already pay for, billed individually.</p>
        </div>
        <div className="pers-price-offer">
          <div className="pers-price-cap">This plan</div>
          <div className="pers-price-value">$49 / mo</div>
          <p className="pers-price-note">All three, in one. A real, verifiable total.</p>
        </div>
      </div>
    </div>
  </div>

  <div className="pers-line">
    <div className="pers-line-col ok">
      <div className="pers-line-tag"><span className="pers-material" aria-hidden="true">check_circle</span> Recommended</div>
      <p className="pers-line-text">Contextualize a price against a true reference: a real alternative cost, a genuine higher tier, or the value returned. <strong>Every number on screen is one the user could verify.</strong></p>
    </div>
    <div className="pers-line-col avoid">
      <div className="pers-line-tag"><span className="pers-material" aria-hidden="true">cancel</span> Avoid</div>
      <p className="pers-line-text">A struck-through "original" that never sold, or a fake premium tier built only to make the real one look cheap. <strong>A reference the user cannot verify is a lie.</strong></p>
    </div>
  </div>
</div>

<div className="pers-section">
  <h2 className="pers-section-title">Notes and Sources</h2>
  <ul className="pers-sources-list">
    <li>
      <strong>1 · Smart defaults.</strong> The mechanism is effort-avoidance and status-quo bias, not "decision fatigue": the ego-depletion account behind that phrase failed a large multi-lab replication (Hagger et al., 2016). A default harms when it is not the choice an attentive user would make, so where there is no majority choice or the stakes are personal, leave it unset. Johnson &amp; Goldstein, "Do Defaults Save Lives?" Science 302 (2003); Thaler &amp; Sunstein, Nudge (2008); NN/g form design.
    </li>
    <li>
      <strong>2 · Endowed progress.</strong> The lift depends on an honest reason for the head start; steps the user never took read as a trick once noticed. Count only real progress: a confirmed email, a saved draft, a completed step. Hull (1932); Kivetz, Urminsky &amp; Zheng, JMR 43 (2006); Nunes &amp; Dreze, "The Endowed Progress Effect," J. Consumer Research 32 (2006).
    </li>
    <li>
      <strong>3 · Reciprocity.</strong> Regan's effect is well established for offline, interpersonal compliance; "value-first content raises product sign-ups" is a reasonable but untested extrapolation, not something Regan or Cialdini measured. <span className="pers-spa">SPA</span> Cialdini, Influence (1984); Regan, J. Experimental Social Psychology 7 (1971).
    </li>
    <li>
      <strong>4 · IKEA and endowment.</strong> The effect needs successful completion: unfinished or trivially easy building does not raise valuation. Whether self-customization specifically lowers sign-up abandonment is an inferential step beyond these studies. <span className="pers-spa">SPA</span> Norton, Mochon &amp; Ariely, J. Consumer Psychology 22 (2012); Kahneman, Knetsch &amp; Thaler, J. Political Economy 98 (1990).
    </li>
    <li>
      <strong>5 · Loss aversion (contested).</strong> Treat it as real but bounded, not a universal law. Gal &amp; Rucker (2018) argue the asymmetry is weaker and context-dependent; Simonson (2018) defends a contingent version. Use loss framing only where a genuine loss exists, and never assume fear out-pulls a clearly stated gain. Kahneman &amp; Tversky, "Prospect Theory," Econometrica 47 (1979); Gal &amp; Rucker, J. Consumer Psychology 28 (2018); Simonson (2018).
    </li>
    <li>
      <strong>6 · Contrast and anchoring.</strong> Anchoring is potent, which is why the reference must be true: a comparison price never charged crosses from context into deception and, in many markets, unlawful pricing. Tversky &amp; Kahneman, Science 185 (1974); Ariely, Loewenstein &amp; Prelec, "Coherent Arbitrariness," Q. J. Economics 118 (2003).
    </li>
    <li>
      <strong>Dark patterns.</strong> Brignull originated the term (deceptive.design); Gray et al. formalized the taxonomy ("The Dark (Patterns) Side of UX Design," CHI 2018); NN/g tracks it as deceptive patterns. The strict truth-and-reversibility gate around each technique is this system's house doctrine, not a claim from the cited authors. <span className="pers-spa">SPA</span>
