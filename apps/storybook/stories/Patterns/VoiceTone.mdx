<div className="vt-section">

  <h2 className="vt-section-title">Three Principles That Never Bend</h2>
  <p className="vt-section-sub">
    These hold on every surface and in every state. When a line is hard to write, it is usually because one of these three is being tested. Return to them and the sentence resolves.

<div className="vt-principle">
  <div className="vt-principle-head">
    <span className="vt-num">1</span>
    <h2 className="vt-principle-title">Clarity Over Creativity</h2>
  </div>
  <p className="vt-lede">
    A clever line that makes someone pause to decode it has already failed. The job of interface copy is to let a person act correctly without thinking about the words at all. Write for scanning, not reading: put the outcome first, cut every word that does not change what the user does, and prefer the plain term to the impressive one. If a label and a joke fight, the label wins.
  </p>
  <div className="vt-evidence">
    <span className="vt-material" aria-hidden="true">science</span>
    <div className="vt-evidence-body">
      <strong>People do not read online, they scan.</strong> Nielsen Norman Group's early study found 79 percent of users always scanned a new page and only 16 percent read word by word; concise, scannable, front-loaded copy measured up to 124 percent more usable than the conventional version. Users fixate on headings, links, and the first words of a line in an F-shaped pattern. The UK government's design manuals reach the same rule from public-service scale: write so content is understandable at a reading age of around nine, because clear beats clever for everyone, expert and novice alike.
      <span className="vt-cite">nngroup.com/articles/how-users-read-on-the-web · nngroup.com/articles/concise-scannable-and-objective-how-to-write-for-the-web · design.homeoffice.gov.uk/accessibility/written-content/readability</span>
    </div>
  </div>
  <p className="vt-lede">
    In practice: headline the result ("Saved" before "Your changes were successfully saved"), name buttons for what they do ("Delete event", not "Confirm"), and drop filler like "please", "simply", and "in order to". The U.S. plain language guidance puts the discipline in one line worth taping to the wall: write for your reader, use common words, keep sentences short, and lead with what matters.
  </p>
</div>

<div className="vt-principle">
  <div className="vt-principle-head">
    <span className="vt-num">2</span>
    <h2 className="vt-principle-title">Human, Not Robotic</h2>
  </div>
  <p className="vt-lede">
    Machine copy hides behind the passive voice and the system's point of view: "an error occurred", "your request could not be processed". Human copy speaks in the first and second person, takes responsibility, and sounds like one careful person helping another. Address the reader as "you", let the product say "we", use contractions, and prefer the active voice so it is always clear who does what.
  </p>
  <div className="vt-evidence">
    <span className="vt-material" aria-hidden="true">science</span>
    <div className="vt-evidence-body">
      <strong>Plain, active, personal language is the documented standard, not a stylistic whim.</strong> GOV.UK instructs writers to use "you" for the user, write in the active voice, write conversationally, and avoid jargon and formal filler. The U.S. plain language guidance, backing the Plain Writing Act, prescribes the same: active voice, personal pronouns, and everyday words. Both exist because passive, impersonal copy measurably slows comprehension and erodes trust.
      <span className="vt-cite">guidance.publishing.service.gov.uk/writing-to-gov-uk-standards · digital.gov/guides/plain-language</span>
    </div>
  </div>
  <p className="vt-lede">
    Human does not mean chatty. Warmth is carried by point of view and plainness, not by exclamation marks or personality for its own sake. The test: read the line aloud. If no real person would say it to another across a desk, rewrite it.
  </p>
</div>

<div className="vt-principle">
  <div className="vt-principle-head">
    <span className="vt-num">3</span>
    <h2 className="vt-principle-title">Honest, Never Hype</h2>
  </div>
  <p className="vt-lede">
    Every number, claim, and countdown on screen must be literally true. Copy does not inflate ("the best", "instantly", "in seconds") what the product cannot guarantee, does not manufacture urgency the situation does not hold, and does not bury a cost in a cheerful phrase. Honesty is also about completeness: tell the user what will happen before they commit, especially when the action spends money, shares data, or cannot be undone.
  </p>
  <div className="vt-evidence">
    <span className="vt-material" aria-hidden="true">verified</span>
    <div className="vt-evidence-body">
      <strong>Content honesty is a content-design responsibility, not just a legal one.</strong> Shopify's Polaris content guidelines set the bar for product language: be clear and accurate, help people act with confidence, and never overstate. Language that exaggerates or pressures crosses into the deceptive-pattern territory that Nielsen Norman Group and deceptive.design catalogue as harm. Truthful copy is the cheapest trust the product will ever buy.
      <span className="vt-cite">polaris.shopify.com/content/voice-and-tone · nngroup.com/articles/deceptive-patterns</span>
    </div>
  </div>
  <p className="vt-lede">
    This principle connects directly to the ethics section below and to <strong>Patterns / Persuasion</strong>, where the line between honest persuasion and manipulation is drawn technique by technique. The voice never sells with a claim the user could catch out.
  </p>
</div>

<div className="vt-section">
  <div className="vt-section-label">Reference</div>
  <h2 className="vt-section-title">Microcopy, Pattern by Pattern</h2>
  <p className="vt-section-sub">
    Microcopy is the small text that does the heavy lifting: button labels, field hints, empty states, error lines, confirmations. It is the highest-leverage writing in the product because it sits exactly where a person is deciding what to do. Each pattern below pairs what to say with what to avoid, and why.
  </p>

  <div className="vt-rule-label">Buttons &amp; calls to action</div>
  <p className="vt-section-sub" style={{ marginBottom: '0.25rem' }}>
    A button names its own action so the label reads as a promise of what happens next. Verb plus object beats a vague "Submit"; the exact action beats a generic "Confirm".
  </p>
  <div className="vt-pair">
    <div className="vt-pair-col do">
      <div className="vt-pair-head"><span className="vt-material" aria-hidden="true">check_circle</span> Say this</div>
      <ul className="vt-pair-list">
        <li><span className="say">Create event</span><span className="why">Names the exact outcome of the tap.</span></li>
        <li><span className="say">Delete event</span><span className="why">A destructive action says so, plainly.</span></li>
        <li><span className="say">Save changes</span><span className="why">Verb plus object; no guesswork.</span></li>
        <li><span className="say">Send invites</span><span className="why">The user knows what leaves and to whom.</span></li>
      </ul>
    </div>
    <div className="vt-pair-col dont">
      <div className="vt-pair-head"><span className="vt-material" aria-hidden="true">cancel</span> Not this</div>
      <ul className="vt-pair-list">
        <li><span className="say">Submit</span><span className="why">Submit what, to where? A form word, not an action.</span></li>
        <li><span className="say">Confirm</span><span className="why">On a delete dialog, hides the consequence.</span></li>
        <li><span className="say">OK</span><span className="why">Agrees to nothing in particular.</span></li>
        <li><span className="say">Click here</span><span className="why">Describes the mechanic, not the result.</span></li>
      </ul>
    </div>
  </div>

  <div className="vt-rule-label">Labels &amp; hints</div>
  <p className="vt-section-sub" style={{ marginBottom: '0.25rem' }}>
    A label says what the field is; a hint removes doubt before the user types. Keep both short and specific, and put help where the eye already is, next to the field, not in a distant tooltip.
  </p>
  <div className="vt-pair">
    <div className="vt-pair-col do">
      <div className="vt-pair-head"><span className="vt-material" aria-hidden="true">check_circle</span> Say this</div>
      <ul className="vt-pair-list">
        <li><span className="say">Work email</span><span className="why">Specific enough to prevent the wrong entry.</span></li>
        <li><span className="say">Doors open at</span><span className="why">Plain label paired with an example time.</span></li>
        <li><span className="say">We will only use this to send your tickets.</span><span className="why">Answers the unspoken "why do you need it?".</span></li>
      </ul>
    </div>
    <div className="vt-pair-col dont">
      <div className="vt-pair-head"><span className="vt-material" aria-hidden="true">cancel</span> Not this</div>
      <ul className="vt-pair-list">
        <li><span className="say">Email *</span><span className="why">Which email, and why? The asterisk is not an answer.</span></li>
        <li><span className="say">Time</span><span className="why">Of what? Ambiguous on a busy form.</span></li>
        <li><span className="say">Enter valid input</span><span className="why">Tells the user nothing they can act on.</span></li>
      </ul>
    </div>
  </div>

  <div className="vt-rule-label">Empty states</div>
  <p className="vt-section-sub" style={{ marginBottom: '0.25rem' }}>
    An empty state is not a dead end; it is the first step. Say what will be here, then hand over the one action that fills it. Skip the pep talk and the cleverness.
  </p>
  <div className="vt-pair">
    <div className="vt-pair-col do">
      <div className="vt-pair-head"><span className="vt-material" aria-hidden="true">check_circle</span> Say this</div>
      <ul className="vt-pair-list">
        <li><span className="say">No events yet. Create your first one to get started.</span><span className="why">Names the state, offers the next step.</span></li>
        <li><span className="say">Nothing matches "jazz". Try a broader search.</span><span className="why">Explains the blank and suggests a fix.</span></li>
      </ul>
    </div>
    <div className="vt-pair-col dont">
      <div className="vt-pair-head"><span className="vt-material" aria-hidden="true">cancel</span> Not this</div>
      <ul className="vt-pair-list">
        <li><span className="say">Your story starts now.</span><span className="why">Evocative, but tells the user nothing to do.</span></li>
        <li><span className="say">No results found.</span><span className="why">A dead end with no way forward.</span></li>
      </ul>
    </div>
  </div>

  <div className="vt-rule-label">Errors</div>
  <p className="vt-section-sub" style={{ marginBottom: '0.25rem' }}>
    An error line has one job: get the user unstuck. Say what happened in their words, drop the blame and the codes, and point at the fix. The full anatomy is below.
  </p>
  <div className="vt-pair">
    <div className="vt-pair-col do">
      <div className="vt-pair-head"><span className="vt-material" aria-hidden="true">check_circle</span> Say this</div>
      <ul className="vt-pair-list">
        <li><span className="say">That email address is missing an @. Check and try again.</span><span className="why">Names the problem and the exact fix.</span></li>
        <li><span className="say">This card was declined. Try another card or contact your bank.</span><span className="why">Says what happened and two ways forward.</span></li>
      </ul>
    </div>
    <div className="vt-pair-col dont">
      <div className="vt-pair-head"><span className="vt-material" aria-hidden="true">cancel</span> Not this</div>
      <ul className="vt-pair-list">
        <li><span className="say">Invalid input.</span><span className="why">Which input, invalid how, fixed how? Nothing to act on.</span></li>
        <li><span className="say">Error 402: transaction failed.</span><span className="why">A code and blame, no path out.</span></li>
      </ul>
    </div>
  </div>

  <div className="vt-rule-label">Confirmations</div>
  <p className="vt-section-sub" style={{ marginBottom: '0.25rem' }}>
    Confirm in the fewest words that close the loop. State what happened and, if there is one, the next thing the user might want. Then get out of the way.
  </p>
  <div className="vt-pair">
    <div className="vt-pair-col do">
      <div className="vt-pair-head"><span className="vt-material" aria-hidden="true">check_circle</span> Say this</div>
      <ul className="vt-pair-list">
        <li><span className="say">Event created. It is now visible to your audience.</span><span className="why">Confirms and states the consequence.</span></li>
        <li><span className="say">Invites sent to 12 people.</span><span className="why">Specific, verifiable, done.</span></li>
      </ul>
    </div>
    <div className="vt-pair-col dont">
      <div className="vt-pair-head"><span className="vt-material" aria-hidden="true">cancel</span> Not this</div>
      <ul className="vt-pair-list">
        <li><span className="say">Success!</span><span className="why">Success at what? The exclamation carries no information.</span></li>
        <li><span className="say">Your request has been processed.</span><span className="why">Robotic, and vague about what actually happened.</span></li>
      </ul>
    </div>
  </div>

    <span className="vt-material" aria-hidden="true">draft</span>
    <div className="vt-evidence-body">

<div className="vt-section">
  <div className="vt-section-label">The hardest lines to get right</div>
  <h2 className="vt-section-title">Writing Errors People Can Act On</h2>
  <p className="vt-section-sub">
    Error copy is where voice is tested hardest, because the reader is already frustrated. A good error message answers three questions in order, in the user's language: <strong>what happened, why (if it helps), and how to fix it.</strong> Everything else, blame, apology theatre, error codes, jargon, is noise the user has to wade through to get unstuck.
  </p>

  <div className="vt-anatomy">
    <div className="vt-anatomy-step">
      <div className="vt-anatomy-n">01</div>
      <div className="vt-anatomy-title">What happened</div>
      <p className="vt-anatomy-text">Name the problem in plain words, from the user's side. "This card was declined", not "Payment gateway returned 402".</p>
    </div>
    <div className="vt-anatomy-step">
      <div className="vt-anatomy-n">02</div>
      <div className="vt-anatomy-title">Why, if it helps</div>
      <p className="vt-anatomy-text">Add a reason only when it guides the fix. "The file is over the 10 MB limit" earns its place; an internal stack trace does not.</p>
    </div>
    <div className="vt-anatomy-step">
      <div className="vt-anatomy-n">03</div>
      <div className="vt-anatomy-title">How to fix it</div>
      <p className="vt-anatomy-text">Hand back a next step, ideally more than one. "Try another card or contact your bank." Never leave the user at a wall.</p>
    </div>
  </div>

  <div className="vt-evidence">
    <span className="vt-material" aria-hidden="true">rule</span>
    <div className="vt-evidence-body">
      <strong>Actionable, blame-free errors are a named usability heuristic.</strong> Nielsen's ninth heuristic asks interfaces to help users recognize, diagnose, and recover from errors: express the problem in plain language, be precise, and offer a constructive way out. NN/g's error-message guidelines add the human rules, do not blame the user, avoid uppercase and alarming tone, and place the message next to where the error happened.
      <span className="vt-cite">nngroup.com/articles/ten-usability-heuristics (H9) · nngroup.com/articles/error-message-guidelines</span>
    </div>
  </div>

  <div className="vt-demo">
    <span className="vt-demo-label">Email address</span>
    <input className="vt-demo-input" type="text" defaultValue="ada.example.com" aria-describedby="vt-err-hint" readOnly />

      <span className="vt-material" aria-hidden="true">info</span>
      <span>This email is missing an @. It should look like name@example.com.</span>
    </p>
  </div>
  <p className="vt-section-sub" style={{ marginTop: '1rem' }}>
    The visual half of this pattern, the error border, the message placement, the <code>var(--color-error)</code> token, lives in <strong>Patterns / Form Elements</strong>. Voice and Tone owns the words; Form Elements owns the field they sit in. Written well, the two are indistinguishable to the user: one product, helping them recover.
  </p>
</div>

{/* ETHICS OF COPY                               */}
{/* ============================================ */}

<div className="vt-ethics">
  <h2 className="vt-ethics-title">Copy Has Ethics</h2>
  <p className="vt-ethics-lede">
    Words persuade, which means words can manipulate. The same sentence that helps a person decide can be twisted to trap them. This system draws the line in the copy itself: <strong>no dark patterns, no false urgency, no shame.</strong> A phrase that only works because the user is rushed, confused, or embarrassed is banned, not merely discouraged. The full technique-by-technique treatment lives in <strong>Patterns / Persuasion</strong>; the language rules are here.
  </p>

  <div className="vt-ethics-grid">
    <div className="vt-ethics-card">
      <div className="vt-ethics-card-title"><span className="vt-material" aria-hidden="true">timer_off</span> No false urgency</div>
      <p className="vt-ethics-card-text">No invented countdowns, no "only 2 left" the data cannot back, no fake "selling fast". Urgency is stated only when it is literally true.</p>
    </div>
    <div className="vt-ethics-card">
      <div className="vt-ethics-card-title"><span className="vt-material" aria-hidden="true">sentiment_dissatisfied</span> No confirmshaming</div>
      <p className="vt-ethics-card-text">The decline option is neutral. "No thanks" is a valid answer, never "No, I like paying full price". Guilt is not a button label.</p>
    </div>
    <div className="vt-ethics-card">
      <div className="vt-ethics-card-title"><span className="vt-material" aria-hidden="true">price_check</span> No hidden cost</div>
      <p className="vt-ethics-card-text">Every charge, fee, and commitment is named before the user agrees, in the same breath as the benefit. No cost buried in cheerful phrasing.</p>
    </div>
    <div className="vt-ethics-card">
      <div className="vt-ethics-card-title"><span className="vt-material" aria-hidden="true">visibility</span> No dark patterns</div>

{/* ============================================ */}
{/* TAXONOMY RULES                               */}
{/* ============================================ */}

<div className="vt-section">
  <h2 className="vt-rule-label" style={{ marginTop: 0 }}>Rules of the Voice</h2>

  <div className="vt-rules">
    <div className="vt-rules-col do">
      <div className="vt-rules-col-title"><span className="vt-rules-badge">+</span> Do</div>
      <ul className="vt-rules-list">
        <li>Lead with the outcome; put the point in the first three words.</li>
        <li>Name buttons for their action, verb plus object.</li>
        <li>Write in the active voice, addressing the user as "you".</li>
        <li>Keep it plain enough to read at a glance, jargon-free.</li>
        <li>In errors, say what happened and how to fix it.</li>
        <li>Match the register to the user's moment, not the team's.</li>
        <li>Make every number and claim literally true.</li>
      </ul>
    </div>
    <div className="vt-rules-col dont">
      <div className="vt-rules-col-title"><span className="vt-rules-badge">x</span> Don't</div>
      <ul className="vt-rules-list">
        <li>Trade clarity for a clever line that needs decoding.</li>
        <li>Label actions "Submit", "Confirm", or "OK" when a real verb exists.</li>
        <li>Hide behind the passive voice or the system's point of view.</li>
        <li>Ship "An error occurred" or a raw code to the user.</li>
        <li>Celebrate a destructive action or bury its consequence.</li>
        <li>Manufacture urgency, shame the decline, or hide a cost.</li>
        <li>Add filler: "please", "simply", "in order to", stray exclamation marks.</li>
      </ul>
    </div>
  </div>
</div>

<div className="vt-section">
  <h2 className="vt-rule-label" style={{ marginTop: 0 }}>Before You Ship a Line</h2>

  <div className="vt-decision">
    <div className="vt-decision-step">
      <div className="vt-decision-text"><strong>Can the user act on it at a glance?</strong> If the outcome is not clear in the first few words, rewrite it leading with the result.</div>
    </div>
    <div className="vt-decision-step">
      <div className="vt-decision-text"><strong>Would a real person say this out loud?</strong> Read it aloud. If it sounds like a machine or a form, make it human, active, and personal.</div>
    </div>
    <div className="vt-decision-step">
      <div className="vt-decision-text"><strong>Does the register fit the moment?</strong> Match the user's state, warm at a win, calm at an error, plain and cheerless at a destructive step.</div>
    </div>
    <div className="vt-decision-step">
      <div className="vt-decision-text"><strong>Is every word true and free of pressure?</strong> No invented urgency, no shame, no hidden cost, no claim the user could catch out. If in doubt, see <strong>Patterns / Persuasion</strong>.</div>
    </div>
    <div className="vt-decision-step">
      <div className="vt-decision-text"><strong>Only now, ship it,</strong> in the DS primitives. See <strong>Patterns / Form Elements</strong> for the fields and error states that carry these words.</div>
